How Green is Melbourne?

Melbourne is known for its parks, gardens and leafy streets. However, the city's greenery is not evenly distributed. This visualisation explores Melbourne's urban forest through tree locations, tree species, tree size, life expectancy and future planting priorities.

Where are Melbourne's trees?

The first map shows the location of individual trees recorded by the City of Melbourne. Each point represents one tree. By mapping every tree, we can see where greenery is concentrated and where the urban forest appears more sparse.

This map provides the spatial foundation for the story: Melbourne has many trees, but they are not spread evenly across all precincts.

Where are the largest trees?

Not all trees contribute equally to the feeling of greenness. Larger and more mature trees often provide stronger shade, greater visual impact and more environmental benefits.

This bubble map uses tree diameter to highlight where Melbourne's larger trees are located.

Which precincts are greener?

Maps are useful for location, but bar charts make comparison easier. These charts compare tree counts and average tree diameter across precincts.

A precinct with many trees is not always the same as a precinct with large mature trees.

Tree quantity vs tree maturity

This scatterplot combines two ideas: how many trees a precinct has and how large those trees are on average. Precincts with both high tree count and high average diameter may have a stronger urban forest presence.

What kinds of trees shape Melbourne?

Melbourne's urban forest is made up of many different species. Species diversity matters because it can make the urban forest more resilient to disease, pests and climate stress. It also affects the character of different streets and precincts.

Is Melbourne's urban forest future-ready?

A green city must keep renewing its urban forest. Tree diameter and useful life expectancy help us understand whether Melbourne's trees are young, mature, aging or likely to need replacement in the future.

Useful life expectancy

Useful life expectancy gives a planning view of the urban forest. Areas with many trees in shorter life expectancy categories may need more attention in future planting strategies.

Where will Melbourne plant next?

The second dataset focuses on future planting schedules. This shifts the story from Melbourne's current trees to the city's future greening priorities.

Today vs tomorrow

The final chart brings the two datasets together. It compares current tree counts with future planting records by precinct. This helps identify whether future planting is focused on already green areas or areas that may need more trees.

Overall, Melbourne has a large and diverse urban forest, but its greenery is uneven. The city's future greenness depends on where new trees are planted and whether mature trees can be sustained over time.
